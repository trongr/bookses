Project Timeline

Note

Each of the following stages involves two images, and you should spend approx. half an hour on each one.

Monday 6 PM. Chosen two scenes

Requirements

  • Please choose scenes in both halves of the story. We want to spread the illustrations out so they don't all clump together in the beginning of the story.
  • As much as possible try to choose visually exciting / dynamic / action scenes, where the characters are actually doing something.

Once OK'd, start designing main / recurring characters in those scenes.

Requirements

  • Draw the entire body, what they look like, what they wear, accessories, etc. If your scene happens to show their entire bodies, draw the scene instead of the character designs.
  • Designs can be rough and sketchy, but they need to be in color and have enough details that other artists can work from.
  • If you don't have a well developed style, go for realism, or at least realistic proportions.

Thursday 6 PM. Designs due

I'll go through everyone's designs and choose the best one for each character, and we'll all work from those. I'll take votes into account so vote away :)

Once chosen, start illustrating your two scenes with characters based on the official designs.

Requirements

  • Draw your characters in the same style as the official designs. And the characters must be colored.
  • Please post your illustrations as you finish them. Don't wait until the deadline to submit both images.

Saturday 6 PM. Scenes v1 due

Call dibs on images you want to improve, and start editing.

Requirements

  • Focus on places with the least details, e.g. blank or sketchy areas. Don't overwork an area if there's a blank spot right next to it.

  • The most important thing to remember about editing is:

    Please add actual things rather than artistic things. Examples of actual things: trees, rocks, hills, mountains, birds, light, shadows, waves, boats, clouds, palaces, eyes, etc. Those are great. Examples of artistic things: adjusting saturation, contrast, hues, tones, filters, generic brush patterns, etc. Don't do that. Spend more time thinking about actual things that contribute to the composition. The more things the better.

    Why? Because actual things are (literally) more substantial than special effects, and it's more fun to watch that transformation. E.g. consider this original, and two different edits: actual versus artistic.

Monday 6 PM. Scenes v2 due

By this point the illustrations should be more or less finished, maybe lacking a few details but the bulk of the work should be done. For those that are finished I'll put up a comment to close them. Those that aren't will go on for more editing. Since there'll be more artists than edits I'll be assigning them individually, depending on who's available and willing.
